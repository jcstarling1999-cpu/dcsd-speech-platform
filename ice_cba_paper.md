# Constitutional Issues CBA

## Issue

**Should ICE be allowed to keep using warrantless arrests, broad surveillance, and enforcement at sensitive locations in the name of public safety, or do those tactics go too far and violate constitutional rights?**

## Position

My position is that current ICE tactics have gone too far and are not a good balance between individual rights and the common good. The government does have a real responsibility to enforce immigration law and protect public safety. I am not saying there should be no immigration enforcement at all. But in a constitutional democracy, the government cannot just say "security" and then ignore the Fourth Amendment, due process, and basic limits on state power. If ICE is entering homes without real judicial warrants, sweeping people up without properly showing flight risk, tracking people through massive surveillance systems, and creating fear around schools, hospitals, and churches, then it is not protecting democracy. It is weakening it.

The better balance is this: require judicial warrants for home entries, strictly limit warrantless civil arrests, restore protected-area rules for schools and hospitals, increase oversight of surveillance technology, and require independent review any time a U.S. citizen or lawful resident is wrongfully detained. That approach still allows enforcement, but it puts the Constitution back where it belongs: above the agency, not underneath it.

## Why the Constitution Matters Here

The Fourth Amendment says that "the right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated" and that warrants must be based on probable cause and specifically describe what is being searched or seized (National Archives). That matters a lot here because ICE raids are not just random policy arguments on TV. They involve homes, cars, phones, workplaces, and people's bodies. Those are exactly the areas the Fourth Amendment was written to protect.

This issue also connects to another major constitutional principle: due process. The Fifth Amendment says no person shall be deprived of liberty without due process of law. It says **person**, not just citizen. That is a big deal. A constitutional government is supposed to use fair procedures even when dealing with people who are unpopular or politically easy to target. If the government starts acting like some people deserve weaker rights just because of immigration status, then the whole idea of limited government starts to crack.

The Constitution promotes at least two ideals that connect directly to this issue.

First, it promotes **liberty**. The point of the Fourth Amendment is not to make law enforcement impossible. The point is to stop the government from acting like it can search, seize, or detain first and justify it later. That is why neutral judges exist. If the same agency that wants to arrest someone can also basically approve its own paperwork and call it good enough, then the check is fake.

Second, it promotes the **rule of law**. In a democracy, even agencies with serious responsibilities still have to follow legal rules. ICE does not get a special pass just because immigration is politically heated. If anything, that is when constitutional rules matter more.

## Background: How ICE Got This Much Power

ICE was created after 9/11 through the Homeland Security Act of 2002, which reorganized federal immigration and customs enforcement inside the new Department of Homeland Security (Congress.gov). Since then, the agency's mission has expanded way past ports of entry and border checkpoints into interior enforcement in neighborhoods, job sites, apartment complexes, and local jails.

That post-9/11 history matters because immigration started getting framed less as a policy issue and more as a security issue. Once that happened, aggressive tactics became easier to defend politically. But the Constitution did not disappear after 9/11. The Bill of Rights was not written only for calm times. It was written for moments when the government feels tempted to push past limits.

There is also a legal reason this history matters. Immigration enforcement is technically civil, not criminal, but the consequences can still be extreme: detention, family separation, removal from the country, and long-term trauma. That creates a weird constitutional zone where the government often tries to claim broad power because the process is "civil," while the people affected experience it as something just as serious as criminal punishment. NYU Law Review has argued that once immigration detention starts functioning like domestic crime control, due process requires stronger procedural safeguards instead of automatic judicial deference (Kreimer). That makes sense to me. If the state is using detention and surveillance in a way that feels punitive, then it should not get to hide behind the label "civil" and avoid real constitutional review.

## What the Legal Debate Actually Is

One thing that makes this topic confusing is that people throw around the word "warrant" like all warrants are basically the same. They are not. A judicial warrant is approved by a neutral judge. An administrative warrant is internal agency paperwork. That difference is not a tiny technicality. It goes to the heart of the Fourth Amendment because the whole point is that the government is not supposed to be its own judge.

That is why recent law review writing matters here. In *Lunn v. Commonwealth*, covered by the *Harvard Law Review*, the Massachusetts high court rejected the idea that local officers had some vague "inherent authority" to make warrantless arrests for civil immigration violations. The review explained that part of the problem was the lack of the ordinary safeguards that usually come with arrests, especially prompt probable-cause review by a neutral magistrate (Harvard Law Review). That case focused on local officers and detainers, but the reasoning still matters more broadly: when the state is making a civil arrest that looks and feels like a criminal arrest, constitutional safeguards cannot just disappear.

A different but related legal issue is who counts for Fourth Amendment purposes. The government often acts like noncitizens, especially in immigration settings, are standing outside the Constitution. But Fourth Amendment scholarship in the *Yale Law Journal* argues that constitutional protections cannot be reduced to a neat citizen/noncitizen split, especially when government searches pull in data, communications, or homes connected to people inside the United States (Yale Law Journal). Even if courts do not give every noncitizen the exact same level of protection in every context, the bigger point still stands: immigration status does not automatically erase the Constitution.

## Evidence That Current ICE Tactics Are Breaking Constitutional Principles

One reason I take this position is that verified evidence shows ICE enforcement is not just targeting the "worst of the worst." The Government Accountability Office found that from fiscal year 2015 through the second quarter of fiscal year 2020, available ICE data showed the agency arrested 674 potential U.S. citizens, detained 121, and removed 70 potential U.S. citizens (GAO). Even if every one of those cases gets called a "mistake," that is still a serious constitutional problem. A government agency with the power to detain and deport should not be this sloppy when citizenship is on the line.

Another reason is that recent federal court rulings suggest ICE has not been following the law even under existing immigration statutes. In Colorado, a federal judge issued a preliminary injunction on November 25, 2025, blocking ICE from making warrantless arrests unless officers have probable cause both that a person is removable and that the person is likely to escape before a warrant can be obtained (ACLU of Colorado). In Washington, D.C., U.S. District Judge Beryl Howell issued a similar preliminary injunction on December 2, 2025, after finding a likely unlawful policy and practice of warrantless civil immigration arrests without the required probable cause and escape-risk findings (*The Guardian*). Those rulings matter because they show this is not just activists being dramatic online. Federal judges looked at the facts and decided ICE likely crossed legal lines.

There is also a policy problem with "protected" or "sensitive" locations. The National Immigration Law Center reported that the Trump administration rescinded the protected areas policy on January 20, 2025, removing prior safeguards for schools, hospitals, and places of worship and replacing them with weak, internal guidance (NILC). That change may sound bureaucratic, but the real-world effect is obvious: when people think a hospital or school could become an enforcement zone, they stop going. At that point, the harm spreads way beyond the individual person ICE is trying to arrest.

Another reason the current system looks unconstitutional is the way it normalizes prolonged detention with weak process. The more the government leans on detention as a regular management tool instead of a limited last resort, the more it drifts away from due process and toward punishment by bureaucracy. That is exactly why the legal line between civil detention and criminal punishment matters so much. If someone loses freedom for months while trying to challenge the government's evidence, the constitutional answer cannot just be "well, technically this is administrative."

## Public Health and Community Harm

This is where the "common good" part of the assignment really matters. Supporters of aggressive ICE tactics say they are necessary to protect the public. But if the tactics themselves make communities less safe and less healthy, then that argument starts to fall apart.

Research backs that up. An American Journal of Public Health study on local immigration enforcement in North Carolina found that tougher enforcement created barriers to care and negatively affected prenatal health access for Hispanic and Latina women (Rhodes et al.; Wake Forest Baptist summary of the AJPH study). A later PLOS ONE study by Duke researchers found that heightened immigration enforcement in North Carolina reduced birth weight by about 58.54 grams and worsened prenatal care patterns for exposed families, including U.S.-citizen children (Tome et al.). That means the fallout does not stop with the person ICE is targeting. It spills into babies, families, and public health.

The mental-health evidence is bad too. A 2025 **JAMA Network Open** study found high levels of poor health, mental illness, and PTSD among recently detained immigrants, with especially severe outcomes for people detained six months or longer. The PTSD rate in that study was 59.3% for people detained at least six months, compared with 34.8% for those detained less than six months (JAMA Network Open). The American Academy of Pediatrics has also warned that even short periods of detention can cause psychological trauma and long-term mental health risks for children (AAP).

So when people say harsh enforcement is for the common good, I think that claim has to be tested against actual evidence. If families avoid prenatal care, children experience trauma, and people lose trust in schools and hospitals, that is not some abstract side effect. That is public harm.

The damage also reaches people who are never detained themselves. Research on fear of deportation found that people with greater deportation fear were less likely to trust police, less likely to believe Latinos would be treated fairly, and less likely to report crimes (Becerra et al.). A 2022 *Journal of Urban Economics* study found that after the federal Priority Enforcement Program shifted attention away from broad immigration-related offenses and toward serious crimes, crime reporting by Hispanics in Dallas increased by 4% (Journal of Urban Economics). That is a huge point for this paper. It suggests the problem is not "immigrant communities just do not trust police for no reason." Policy choices shape that trust. When enforcement gets more targeted and less dragnet-style, cooperation rises.

That matters for the common good because public safety is not only about arrests. It is also about whether victims call 911, whether witnesses talk, and whether families feel safe going to court, school, or the emergency room. A system that scares people away from institutions they need is not protecting society very well.

## Surveillance Makes the Problem Bigger

The issue is not only raids. It is also the surveillance system behind them. In April 2025, *Wired* reported that ICE was paying Palantir $30 million to build a new platform called ImmigrationOS, designed to give the agency near real-time visibility into self-deportation and help prioritize deportation targets, especially visa overstays (Haskins). This is important because the Constitution is not only about doors getting kicked in. It is also about whether the government can build huge digital systems that combine personal data, target people at scale, and then claim it is just efficient administration.

That kind of system makes it easier for the government to cast a wide net instead of using individualized suspicion. It also increases the chances of data errors, false matches, and wrongful detention. If ICE already has a record of detaining potential U.S. citizens, giving it even bigger surveillance tools without stronger safeguards seems backwards.

The surveillance piece gets worse when you look at how DHS and ICE gather data. The Brennan Center reported in 2023 that internal DHS records showed widespread use of fake social media accounts and weak privacy rules around social media surveillance (Brennan Center, "Documents Reveal Widespread Use"). The Brennan Center has also warned that federal agencies, including parts of DHS, buy consumer and location data from private brokers, creating a path around normal warrant requirements (Brennan Center, "Federal Agencies Are Secretly Buying Consumer Data"). In plain terms, the government is not only watching people more. It is also buying its way around the kind of oversight that would normally protect privacy.

That should bother more than just immigrants. If the government gets used to using data brokers, fake accounts, dragnet monitoring, and massive analytics in one area, those tools do not stay locked in one box forever. History shows that surveillance methods almost always expand once institutions get comfortable with them.

## Washington State Shows the Federalism Side

Because I live in Washington, I think it is important to show that this issue is not just national. It is local too. Washington's Keep Washington Working Act is based on a pretty simple constitutional idea: the federal government can enforce federal immigration law, but it cannot force state and local governments to do that job for it. Washington's Attorney General explains that the law exists to protect rights and dignity while keeping local resources focused on actual state and local public safety work (Washington Attorney General, "Immigration Guidance").

That matters because one of the strongest arguments from the other side is based on federal supremacy. People say immigration is a federal issue, so states should just cooperate. But constitutional federalism is more complicated than that. States generally cannot block federal officers from enforcing federal law, but they also do not have to spend their own resources helping with civil immigration enforcement. That anti-commandeering principle is a real part of the constitutional structure.

Washington officials have leaned into that. In March 2025, the Washington Attorney General sued Adams County, arguing that county officials illegally helped federal immigration enforcement by holding people based on immigration status and sharing confidential information in violation of state law (Washington Attorney General, "Washington state sues Adams County"). Cascade PBS reported similar state-level fights over courthouse information-sharing and enforcement around courts (Cascade PBS). That local evidence matters for this paper because it shows something important: protecting constitutional values is not only a job for federal judges. States can also draw lines to protect community trust, due process, and local self-government.

## Washington Examples Make the Harm Feel Real

The Spokane protest in June 2025 is one example of how fast this issue can blow up in a real community. According to the *Spokesman-Review*, two Venezuelan men who had entered through humanitarian parole and were following the legal process were detained when they showed up for an immigration check-in, which triggered a mass protest and major police response in Spokane (Spokesman-Review, June 12, 2025). Months later, the same outlet reported that a federal judge ruled one of those detentions illegal and said the government had denied the asylum seeker due process, calling the detention an abuse of discretion (Spokesman-Review, Feb. 3, 2026).

That story matters for more than local politics. It shows how quickly "just enforcing the law" can turn into a due-process problem, a public-trust crisis, and a citywide conflict all at once. Once that happens, it is not only the detained person who pays. Schools, police, city government, churches, and neighbors all get pulled into the damage.

## The Economic Side of the Common Good

The "common good" is not just about crime. It is also about whether policies help communities function. Pew Research found that unauthorized immigrant workers made up especially large shares of the workforce in construction and agriculture in 2022, sectors that many local economies depend on (Pew Research Center). That does not mean labor law should disappear. It does mean mass-sweep enforcement has ripple effects on housing, food production, and local businesses.

The American Immigration Council estimated in 2024 that a one-time mass deportation operation targeting 13.3 million people would cost at least $315 billion, and a plan to deport one million people per year would average $88 billion annually (American Immigration Council). That is not fiscally small. If the government is spending massive amounts of money on detention beds, surveillance systems, transport, and raids while also damaging local economies, then it is fair to ask whether that really serves the public.

Washington reporting makes this feel less abstract. In Spokane, two immigrants who were reportedly in legal processes were detained at a routine ICE check-in in June 2025, which helped spark mass protest and a huge law-enforcement response (Spokesman-Review, June 12, 2025). Whether someone agrees with every protest tactic or not, the bigger point is that aggressive enforcement can tear up trust in a community fast.

There is also a bigger budget-priority issue. The Economic Policy Institute reported that immigration enforcement has been funded at much higher levels than labor standards enforcement and that the gap keeps widening (Economic Policy Institute, 2022). EPI also projected major employment effects from mass deportation, including significant losses in construction employment and a 4.0% overall employment impact in Washington under a one-million-deportations-per-year scenario (Economic Policy Institute, 2025 figures). That matters because a government saying it cares about the common good should have to explain why it keeps pouring resources into punishment-heavy systems while underfunding labor protections, wage enforcement, and basic worker safety.

So for me, the economic argument is not just "raids are mean." It is that they are also expensive, disruptive, and often self-defeating.

## Historical Comparisons Matter Too

I do not think history should be used in a lazy way where every bad government policy gets called the same thing. But I do think history matters because America has a pattern of relaxing civil liberties during periods of fear and then regretting it later.

The Palmer Raids are one example. *History.com* explains that thousands of people were swept up in raids during the Red Scare, but many of those arrests ignored due process and ended in embarrassment once the panic cooled down (History.com). The basic pattern sounds familiar: fear gets turned into broad executive power, people get treated as collective threats instead of individuals, and legal safeguards are treated like obstacles.

The Chinese Exclusion era is another example. PBS's *American Experience* notes that the Chinese Exclusion Act was the first major federal law to ban a specific ethnic group's immigration and stayed in force for more than sixty years (PBS). In other words, the U.S. has done this before: create a racialized immigration panic, build harsh systems around it, and then normalize those systems for decades. That does not prove every current ICE action is identical to past abuses, but it does show that "this is temporary" and "this is only for security" are not very trustworthy excuses.

History matters in this CBA because the Constitution is supposed to be the thing that slows panic down. If we only honor constitutional limits after the fear passes, then we are honoring them way too late.

## Evaluation of a Government Policy and Court Response

The government policy I am evaluating is ICE's recent use of broad warrantless civil arrests and its move away from stronger limits at protected areas. In my view, that policy did a poor job of upholding constitutional principles.

From the government's side, the strongest argument is that immigration enforcement is a federal responsibility and officers need flexibility. Officials also argue that some people already have final orders of removal or that immigration law allows warrantless arrests in limited cases. That is the fairest version of the other side: the government cannot do its job if every step is slowed down.

But that argument only works if ICE actually follows the legal limits built into the law. The Colorado and D.C. injunctions show why this matters. Those courts did not say immigration law can never be enforced. They said ICE cannot skip the specific findings the law requires for warrantless arrests. In other words, the court response upheld the constitutional principle better than the policy did. The policy leaned toward executive convenience. The injunctions pushed the government back toward individualized suspicion, documentation, and accountability.

So my evaluation is this: the policy failed because it normalized shortcuts. The courts partly corrected that failure by reminding ICE that civil immigration enforcement still has legal boundaries. That is exactly how checks and balances are supposed to work.

I also think Washington's Keep Washington Working framework shows a second kind of constitutional response. Instead of waiting for federal courts to fix everything after the fact, the state tried to prevent harm by limiting how local agencies participate in federal civil immigration enforcement in the first place. That approach is not anti-Constitution. If anything, it reflects constitutional federalism and the idea that local law enforcement should stay focused on state and local safety, not become an extension of a federal civil-deportation machine.

## A Fair Counterargument

A fair counterargument is that strong ICE enforcement protects the common good by removing dangerous people, discouraging illegal immigration, and showing that federal law actually means something. A lot of people also believe sanctuary policies go too far the other way and make it harder to remove people who really are dangerous. That concern is not fake. Public safety is a real constitutional interest too.

I agree with one part of that argument: the government should be able to remove people who commit serious crimes and it should be able to enforce immigration law in a targeted, lawful way.

But that is not the same thing as defending the current system. A system that wrongfully detains potential U.S. citizens, scares people away from hospitals and schools, and relies on warrantless arrests that federal judges are blocking is not a narrow public-safety tool anymore. It is overbroad. Even if the goal is legal enforcement, the method still has to be constitutional.

That is why I reject the counterargument as a full defense of current ICE tactics. It treats any limit on enforcement as weakness, when really those limits are the difference between constitutional government and government overreach.

I also think the counterargument sometimes mixes up being "tough" with being effective. The Dallas evidence on crime reporting suggests that when enforcement gets narrower and more focused on serious threats, public cooperation can actually improve (Journal of Urban Economics). So even if someone's main value is public safety, there is still a strong reason to reject overly broad raids and surveillance.

## How to Advocate for This Position

If I were advocating for this position in real life, I would not just say "abolish everything" and leave it there. I would push for specific changes that people can actually organize around.

First, Congress should require judicial warrants for home entries in civil immigration cases and tighten the standards for warrantless arrests. If the government wants to take someone's liberty, it should have to show its evidence to a neutral judge whenever possible.

Second, DHS should restore and strengthen protected-area rules for schools, hospitals, and places of worship. Those are places where access matters to the whole community, not just to one person.

Third, there should be mandatory public reporting and independent investigations whenever ICE wrongfully detains a U.S. citizen or causes serious injury or death during an operation. If there is no transparency, then "oversight" is just a slogan.

Fourth, state and local governments can limit how much local systems feed federal surveillance and detention pipelines. They can also fund legal aid, rapid-response networks, and know-your-rights education so constitutional rights are not just theoretical.

For students, advocacy can look smaller but still real: contacting representatives, speaking at school board or city council meetings, writing op-eds, supporting local legal aid groups, and learning the difference between administrative warrants and judicial warrants. Constitutional rights only work if regular people know what they are.

I would also add a fifth step: Congress and state legislatures should regulate commercial data sales to law enforcement. If agencies can just buy location, utility, and social-media-related data instead of getting judicial approval, then Fourth Amendment protections are going to keep getting hollowed out by technology. A right that only exists until the government can purchase around it is not much of a right.

## Conclusion

ICE exists because the federal government has authority over immigration. That part is real. But having authority is not the same thing as having unlimited power. The Fourth Amendment, due process, and the rule of law are supposed to stop the government from deciding that some people's rights matter less.

After looking at the evidence, I think current ICE tactics are not a healthy balance between individual rights and the common good. They go too far. Recent federal court injunctions in Colorado and Washington, D.C., the GAO's findings on potential U.S. citizens caught in enforcement, public-health research on prenatal care and trauma, and the growth of large surveillance tools all point in the same direction: the government has been pushing past constitutional limits and communities are paying the price.

If we let the Constitution bend every time the government says a group is dangerous, temporary, or undeserving, then those protections stop being real. And once that happens, it never stays contained to one group for long.
